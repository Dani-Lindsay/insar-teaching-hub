# New Zealand InSAR Papers

!!! warning "Disclaimer"
    This page was assembled by Claude (Anthropic) drawing on Dani Lindsay's thesis work, research notes, and guidance. It has not yet been through a final review — if you see this notice, treat the content as a useful starting point but verify anything you plan to cite or act on.

---

InSAR and geodesy papers relevant to New Zealand — covering active faults, the Hikurangi subduction zone, Taupo Volcanic Zone, landslides, and urban subsidence. One-sentence takeaways are Dani's interpretations.

*Maintained by Danielle Lindsay.*

---

| Year | Authors | Paper | DOI | Takeaway |
|------|---------|-------|-----|----------|
| 2016 | Langridge et al. | *The New Zealand Active Faults Database* | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1080/00288306.2015.1112818) | The reference catalogue for active fault geometry in NZ — the starting point for any InSAR study targeting fault creep or coseismic deformation. |
| 2016 | Haghshenas Haghighi & Motagh | *Assessment of ground surface displacement in Taihape landslide, New Zealand, with C- and X-band SAR interferometry* | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1080/00288306.2015.1127824) | C- and X-band comparison for a slow NZ landslide — useful context on what different wavelengths can resolve at typical NZ landslide velocities. |
| 2020 | Wallace | *Slow slip events in New Zealand* | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-earth-071719-055104) | Comprehensive review of Hikurangi slow slip — essential background for anyone working on the subduction zone or transient signals in NZ InSAR data. |
| 2021 | Hamling | *InSAR observations over the Taupō Volcanic Zone's cone volcanoes: insights and challenges from the New Zealand volcano supersite* | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1080/00288306.2020.1779848) | Honest account of what InSAR can and cannot detect across NZ's arc volcanoes — including the decorrelation and atmospheric noise challenges specific to this environment. |
| 2022 | Bruce | *Version 1 of the New Zealand landslide fatalities database, 1760–2020* | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.21420/SW43-TJ28) | Historical record of NZ landslide fatalities — useful context for framing the hazard significance of slow-moving landslide monitoring. |
| 2022 | Hamling et al. | *A snapshot of New Zealand's dynamic deformation field from Envisat InSAR and GNSS observations between 2003 and 2011* | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1029/2021GL096465) | Baseline interseismic velocity field for NZ from Envisat C-band — a key reference for what the background deformation field looks like before starting any new study. |
| 2023 | Cao et al. | *Slow-moving landslides triggered by the 2016 Mw 7.8 Kaikōura earthquake, New Zealand: a new InSAR phase-gradient based time-series approach* | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1029/2022GL102064) | Phase-gradient time series for detecting co- and post-seismic landslide accelerations across the Kaikōura rupture zone — methodologically useful for triggered-slip studies. |
| 2025 | Kearse et al. | *InSAR measurement of vertical land motion in New Zealand cities, and implications for sea-level rise projections* | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1080/00288306.2024.2354076) | Sentinel-1 vertical land motion in NZ urban centres — shows both subsidence and uplift signals, directly relevant to sea-level hazard assessment. |
| 2025 | McGrath et al. | *Linking geodetically resolved uplift to long-term orogenic exhumation in the Southern Alps, New Zealand* | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1029/2024JB030625) | Connects InSAR-measured rock uplift across the Southern Alps to million-year exhumation rates — a good example of InSAR bridging geodetic and geological timescales. |
| in press | Lindsay et al. | *Nine-year L-band InSAR time series of tectonic and non-tectonic surface deformation in northern California* | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1029/2025JB032718) | ALOS-2 ScanSAR methodology — ionospheric correction, split-spectrum, multi-track velocity fields. Northern California data, but the methods are directly applicable to NZ L-band work. |
